'Graphic Novel: Motherhood in Crisis' Reflection

After reading Tetteh: The prisoner, My punishment, it just pisses me off that she is still incarcerating for something that clearly was self-defense. Yet, her case reminds me of so many other people whose cases are buried deep within the court justice system. That to me is such bullshit, that it takes months, even years for courts to open a case that was made months, hell even years. Not only that but how Female Correctional Centre operates, how they leave these women all by themselves is to essential rot away in their cells. Even with cases like Tetteh, these prisons were not prepared with enough resources. This may be due to a lack of funding.

When reading Heela: The schoolgirl, My stolen future, at first, she did not want to have the child and wanted an abortion. Her reason was that she was still a child who went to school and dreamed of becoming a nurse. But she felt that all of this would be taken away if she gave birth. This also meant that she would have to take care of both the baby and her grandmother who was not able to work, therefore, Heela had to take the role of caretaker. She had a father but because he had three other children to take care of, Heela was no longer a priority for him anymore. Yet the one thing that surprised me was when Heela does come around with the fact, she is going to have a baby, she stated that if she had a boy, she would name him after her father, and it is because he contributed to her creation and the father would later provide a home for Heela and her child.

When reading Memunatu: The distant patient, Birth in my village, it shows how important it is that people like Memunatu have the proper medical care to make sure both the mother and the newborn are safe after the pregnancy. If not, these women are left with serious that can lead them to their death if given birth the second time. Not only that but it can also leave these women in a state where they are unable to eat, unable to go to the bathroom, and having this feeling they can no longer give birth.

Finally, when reading the Kadiatu: The grieving mother, The devil at my door, this story also serves an example that is important to have a professional opinion instead of listening to “quack doctor.” The one thing all these women in these stories have a common is the fear of going to a health care facility. In most of these stories, the spread of Ebola leads to many like Kadiatu having this fear as they heard stories of people never coming out once they were admitted. Yet, as Kadiatu story progressed, when her only surviving son Abdul became ill, she had no choice but to go to the “healer” but soon understood that her son case needed professional help and this decision lead to Abdul surviving.
